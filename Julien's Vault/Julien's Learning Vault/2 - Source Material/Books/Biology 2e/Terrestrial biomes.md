# Terrestrial biomes

Section 44.3 of the OpenStax _Biology 2e_ textbook, **"Terrestrial Biomes,"** explores Earth's land-based ecosystems. It explains how global climate patterns—specifically **temperature and precipitation**—dictate the distribution of vegetation, which in turn determines the communities of animals and microorganisms that can survive there.

The text details the distinguishing characteristics, plant adaptations, and climatic profiles of Earth’s eight major terrestrial biomes:

### 1. Tropical Rainforests

- **Climate:** Highly stable temperatures year-round (20°C to 30°C / 68°F to 86°F) and immense annual rainfall (250–450 cm / 8.2–14.8 ft). They experience a constant 12-hour day length.
    
- **Vegetation:** Extreme plant diversity characterized by distinct vertical layering (canopy, understory, and forest floor). Epiphytes (plants growing on other plants) are highly common.
    
- **Key Feature:** Known as the most biodiverse terrestrial biome. Because there is no seasonal leaf-drop, the forest remains evergreen year-round.
    

### 2. Savannas

- **Climate:** Warm, tropical temperatures (24°C to 29°C / 75°F to 84°F) paired with an extensive, severe dry season and a single, intense rainy season.
    
- **Vegetation:** Expansive grasslands dotted with sparse, widely spaced trees.
    
- **Key Feature:** Plants have evolved extensive root systems and fire-resistant bark because this biome relies on recurrent natural fires to maintain its open structure and prevent forest encroachment.
    

### 3. Subtropical Deserts

- **Climate:** Centered around the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn, these regions feature extreme evaporation rates, unpredictable rainfall (frequently less than 30 cm / 12 in annually), and drastic daily temperature swings (often reaching up to 60°C/140°F by day and dropping near 0°C/32°F by night).
    
- **Vegetation:** Perennials are scarce; the landscape is dominated by water-storing succulents (like cacti) and deeply rooted shrubs. Many plants are annuals that quickly sprout, bloom, and die following brief rains.
    
- **Key Feature:** Adaptations focus entirely on water conservation, such as leaves modified into protective spines to reduce surface area, and nocturnal behaviors in wildlife.
    

### 4. Chaparral (Scrub Forest)

- **Climate:** Dominated by a Mediterranean climate characterized by mild, rainy winters and hot, intensely dry summers.
    
- **Vegetation:** Mostly low shrubs and small trees.
    
- **Key Feature:** Many chaparral plants are fire-adapted or fire-dependent; their seeds are designed to remain dormant in the soil until the heat or ash from a wildfire triggers germination.
    

### 5. Temperate Grasslands (Prairies/Steppes)

- **Climate:** Pronounced annual seasonal fluctuations with hot summers and freezing, snowy winters. Annual precipitation is moderate (25–75 cm / 9.8–29.5 in).
    
- **Vegetation:** Dominated by dense grasses with very few trees, which are restricted almost exclusively to riverbanks and streams.
    
- **Key Feature:** The soil is incredibly fertile, packed with deep, organic nutrients from centuries of decomposing grass roots. Like savannas, these ecosystems are maintained by grazing animals and periodic fires.
    

### 6. Temperate Forests

- **Climate:** Defined by distinct four-season cycles with temperatures ranging from below freezing to warm summers. They receive reliable, consistent precipitation (75–150 cm / 29.5–59 in) throughout the year.
    
- **Vegetation:** Dominated by deciduous trees (like beech, maple, oak, and hickory) that drop their leaves every autumn.
    
- **Key Feature:** The seasonal dropping of leaves opens the forest canopy in early spring, allowing sunlight to reach the forest floor and fuel a rich layer of undergrowth before the trees leaf out again.
    

### 7. Boreal Forests (Taiga / Coniferous Forest)

- **Climate:** Characterized by long, sub-freezing, dry winters and short, cool, wet summers. Precipitation is low to moderate, often falling as snow.
    
- **Vegetation:** Dominated by cold-tolerant, evergreen coniferous trees like pines, spruces, and firs.
    
- **Key Feature:** The cone shape of the trees allows heavy snow to slide off without snapping branches. The dark green needles readily absorb minimal spring sunlight, and because they are kept year-round, these trees can begin photosynthesis the moment temperatures warm up without spending energy growing new leaves.
    

### 8. Arctic Tundra

- **Climate:** Extremely cold, arid, and windswept conditions. Average winter temperatures drop below -34°C (-29.2°F), and the brief summer growing season lasts only 6 to 10 weeks.
    
- **Vegetation:** Low to the ground, consisting primarily of lichens, mosses, low shrubs, and short grasses. Trees cannot grow here.
    
- **Key Feature:** The subsoil is defined by **permafrost**—a layer of ground that remains permanently frozen year-round. Permafrost acts as a physical barrier that prevents plant roots from penetrating deeply into the earth and stops water from draining, creating temporary bogs and shallow pools across the landscape during the brief summer thaw.
