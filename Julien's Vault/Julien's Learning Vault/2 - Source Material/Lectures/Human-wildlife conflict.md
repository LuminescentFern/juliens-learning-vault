# Human-wildlife conflict

This course content focuses on the complexities of human-wildlife conflict and the importance of coexistence between humans and animals.

Human-Wildlife Conflict Forms

- Conflicts arise when animals threaten human safety, livelihoods, or property, often leading to lethal retaliation.
- Examples include crop damage by elephants, livestock predation by carnivores, property destruction, attacks on humans or pets, and zoonotic diseases.

Causes and Consequences

- Habitat loss, land use changes, prey depletion, natural disasters, and climate change exacerbate conflicts.
- Conflicts can also create divisions among human groups over how to manage wildlife.

Case Study: Wolves in the United States

- Wolves were eradicated by the 1930s due to livestock protection but were reintroduced in the 1990s under the Endangered Species Act.
- Reintroduction sparked human conflicts, especially with ranchers fearing loss of livelihood and restrictions on hunting.
- Despite opposition, wolf populations have stabilized in some areas, with efforts to promote coexistence through non-lethal methods.

Coexistence Strategies

- Innovative solutions include using beehives to deter elephants from crops and masks to confuse tigers to reduce attacks.
- Collaboration between ranchers and conservationists shows that coexistence is possible with preventive measures rather than killing.

Overall Importance

- Human expansion and climate change will increase wildlife encounters, making coexistence essential for human safety, biodiversity, and ecosystem health.
