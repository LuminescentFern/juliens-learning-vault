# Animals and social problems

This lecture explores how social problems are defined and constructed, particularly in relation to animals.

Defining Social Problems

- Social problems are often seen as conditions that are inherently harmful or bad, undermining people's well-being.
- The objective approach assumes social problems have standard indicators of harm, but this can be problematic as definitions of harm and poverty vary by context and perspective.

The Social Constructionist Approach

- Social problems are seen as socially constructed through interaction and interpretation, not merely objective facts.
- Different people may view the same condition differently, and a social problem emerges when a condition is recognized as troubling and in need of a solution by some group.

Animals as Social Problems

- Animals can be metaphorically linked to social problems, as seen with urban pigeons being labeled as "rats with wings" and targeted for elimination despite their neutral or positive roles elsewhere.
- Certain dog breeds like Pitbulls have been constructed as dangerous or problematic through media portrayals and legal restrictions, illustrating how animals are used symbolically in social problem construction.
