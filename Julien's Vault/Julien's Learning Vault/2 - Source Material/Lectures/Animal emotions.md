# Animal emotions

This lecture explores the evolving scientific understanding of animal emotions and cognition, highlighting the historical and contemporary perspectives on whether and how animals experience emotions.

Historical Perspectives on Animal Emotions

- In 1871, Charles Darwin argued that animals feel emotions such as pleasure, pain, happiness, and misery, emphasizing continuity between humans and other animals.
- The early 20th-century behaviorism movement rejected animal consciousness, viewing animals as stimulus-response machines, but this view shifted starting in the 1970s with research by Jane Goodall and others.

Components and Recognition of Emotions

- Emotions have two key dimensions: physiological responses (e.g., heart rate, adrenaline) and cognitive interpretation (how the emotion is understood and influences behavior).
- If Darwin’s continuity across species holds, other animals likely experience both physiological and cognitive components of emotions.

Anthropomorphism and Animal Emotions

- Describing animal emotions often involves using human language, which can lead to anthropomorphism, but careful interpretation grounded in animal capabilities helps avoid misrepresentation.
- While animal emotions may not be identical to human emotions, evidence supports that animals experience feelings such as contentment, fear, and annoyance.
