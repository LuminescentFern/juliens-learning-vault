# Animals in laboratories (2)

This lecture recounts the story of Pepper, a family Dalmatian stolen and sold to a research lab, which led to significant changes in U.S. animal welfare laws.

The Incident and Public Outcry

- Pepper was taken by a dog dealer and sold to a research hospital, where she died during an experimental procedure.
- Media coverage and public outrage over pet theft and animal mistreatment in research facilities increased awareness and pressure on lawmakers.

Legislative Response and the Animal Welfare Act

- Representative Joseph Resnick introduced a bill requiring licensing and inspection of dog and cat dealers supplying research labs.
- The Laboratory Animal Welfare Act was passed in 1966, regulating the transportation, sale, and handling of animals used in research.

Amendments and Ongoing Regulations

- In 1970, the act was renamed the Animal Welfare Act and expanded to include care standards inside research labs after the Silver Spring monkey case.
- Institutional Animal Care and Use Committees (IACUCs) were established to oversee animal research ethics.
- The 2002 amendment excluded birds, rats, and mice bred for research, but NIH guidelines still regulate their use in federally funded research.
