# The lobster essay

This lecture focuses on David Foster Wallace's essay "Consider the Lobster," which explores the ethical questions surrounding the practice of boiling lobsters alive for food.

Background and Context

- The essay was published in Gourmet magazine in 2004 and later included in a collection of Wallace's essays.
- Its title references M.F.K. Fisher's 1941 book "Consider the Oyster," highlighting the importance of understanding where food comes from and the ethics involved.

Ethical Inquiry into Lobster Cooking

- Wallace attended the Maine Lobster Festival and observed the event with a mix of humor and critical reflection.
- He raises the question of whether it is morally acceptable to boil a sentient creature alive solely for taste pleasure.

Pain and Suffering of Lobsters

- Wallace presents evidence that lobsters likely feel pain when boiled alive, describing their reactions and various cooking methods aimed at minimizing suffering.
- He discusses utilitarian approaches by cooks who try to reduce the lobster's pain while preserving flavor.

Moral Reflection and Conclusion

- The essay encourages readers to engage in self-reflected moral inquiry rather than demanding a specific action like stopping lobster consumption.
- Wallace's main goal is to prompt thoughtful consideration of the justification for causing suffering to animals for gustatory enjoyment.
