# Sámi people keep life focused on reindeer herding

The Mongabay article **"In the Arctic, Indigenous Sámi keep life centered on reindeer herding"** explores how the Sámi people of Sápmi (the Arctic region spanning Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Russia) maintain their ancient, deeply symbiotic relationship with reindeer herding in the face of escalating modern pressures.

The article highlights the cultural, ecological, and economic dimensions of this practice through several key themes:

### 1. Reindeer as a Cultural and Economic Foundation

For the herding Sámi, reindeer are not merely livestock; they are the center of their identity, language, and worldview.

- **The Seasonal Cycle:** The Sámi structure their lives around the natural migrations of the reindeer, moving them between winter forests and summer coastal pastures.
    
- **Cultural Identity:** The practice preserves traditional knowledge, craft (_duodji_), and language. Specialized vocabulary allows herders to describe snow, weather, and reindeer behavior with extreme precision—nuances that are critical for survival in the Arctic.
    

### 2. The Threat of Climate Change

The Arctic is warming at a rate much faster than the global average, throwing centuries of predictable environmental patterns into chaos.

- **The "Rain-on-Snow" Crisis:** Warmer winters cause rain to fall on existing snowpacks. When the temperature drops again, a hard, impenetrable layer of ice forms over the ground. Reindeer cannot dig through this ice to reach the lichen (_tundra moss_) they depend on for winter food, leading to mass starvation unless herders intervene with costly supplemental feeding.
    
- **Unpredictable Infrastructure:** Unpredictable freeze-and-thaw cycles make frozen rivers and lakes unsafe for traditional migration crossings.
    

### 3. Industrial Encroachment and "Green Colonialism"

The article emphasizes that climate change is not the only threat; industrial development increasingly fragments the reindeer's pasturelands.

- **Land Fragmentation:** Logging, mining, tourism, and expanding road networks cut off vital migration routes and reduce the total area available for grazing.
    
- **The Green Dilemma:** Ironically, efforts to transition to renewable energy—such as constructing massive wind farms and building infrastructure for electric vehicles—often take place on ancestral Sámi herding grounds. This conflict is frequently described by Indigenous activists as "green colonialism," where climate mitigation efforts further displace Indigenous communities.
    

### 4. Resilience and the Fight for Rights

Despite these compounding pressures, Sámi herders are actively resisting the loss of their livelihood by blending generational knowledge with modern tools.

- **Legal and Political Battles:** Sámi communities and parliaments are fighting for stronger legal protections over their land rights, demanding a seat at the table for regional development decisions.
    
- **Adaptive Strategies:** Herders are adapting by utilizing technology (such as GPS tracking and snowmobiles) to monitor herds over fragmented landscapes, while remaining steadfastly committed to the core, holistic values of Traditional Ecological Knowledge.
