# The birds of Shakespeare cause US trouble

### 1. The Romantic Origin Story

In 1890, a wealthy New York drug manufacturer and eccentric Shakespeare devotee named Eugene Schieffelin embarked on a mission to introduce every bird species mentioned in the works of William Shakespeare to North America.

- **The Release:** Schieffelin released approximately 60 to 100 European Starlings into Central Park, New York.
    
- **The Literary Catalyst:** The entire introduction stems from a single line in Shakespeare's play _Henry IV, Part 1_, where the character Hotspur fantasizes about using a talking bird to drive his enemy crazy, stating: _"I'll have a starling shall be taught to speak nothing but 'Mortimer'."_
    

### 2. The Population Explosion

While many of Schieffelin’s other introduced species failed to adapt, the starlings thrived remarkably.

- From that initial handful of birds released in New York, the population exploded exponentially over the next century.
    
- Today, there are an estimated **200 million starlings** across North America, spanning all the way from Alaska to Mexico.
    
- Their extreme biological success is due to their opportunistic feeding habits, high reproductive rates, and flexible habitat requirements.
    

### 3. Systemic and Ecological Impact

What began as an aesthetic tribute to literature quickly transformed into a major ecological and economic catastrophe. The article highlights three primary areas of impact:

#### A. Ecological Disruption

Starlings are highly aggressive "bully birds." They fiercely compete with native North American cavity-nesting birds—such as bluebirds, owls, and woodpeckers—for prime nesting real estate, frequently driving native populations out of their local habitats.

#### B. Agricultural Damage

Massive flocks of starlings cause hundreds of millions of dollars in agricultural destruction annually. They systematically devastate fruit orchards, consume vast quantities of grain, and contaminate cattle feed and water systems with their droppings, which can spread transmissible diseases to livestock.

#### C. Aviation Safety Hazards

Because starlings travel in dense, tightly packed flocks numbering in the thousands (called _murmuration_ loops), they pose a severe hazard to aircraft. When a massive flock collides with a plane, the birds can get sucked into jet engines, causing catastrophic engine failure. The article notes that a notorious 1960 crash in Boston caused by a flock of starlings resulted in 62 fatalities.

### 4. Cultural Legacy

The starling invasion stands as one of history's most famous cautionary tales regarding **biological invasion** and the unintended, permanent systemic consequences of human ecological manipulation. It proves how a seemingly harmless, poetic gesture can permanently alter the biodiversity and landscape of an entire continent.
