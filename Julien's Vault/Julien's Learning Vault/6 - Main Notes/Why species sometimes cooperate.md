# Why species sometimes cooperate

This lecture focuses on the biology and ecology of corals, emphasizing their complex relationships with other organisms and their role in marine ecosystems.

Corals and Their Symbiotic Algae

- Corals are animals that host tiny algae called symbiodiniaceae within their tissues, which perform photosynthesis and provide energy to the coral.
- This mutualistic relationship allows corals to grow large limestone structures, while algae receive protection and access to sunlight.

Coral Bleaching and Environmental Stress

- Coral bleaching occurs when environmental stress, such as warming oceans or sedimentation, causes algae to leave the coral, making the coral appear white.
- The breakdown of this mutualism threatens coral health and reef ecosystems.

Microorganisms and Coral Health

- Besides algae, corals host bacteria and viruses, some of which infect the symbiotic algae and may influence coral resilience or vulnerability.
- Research is ongoing to understand how these microorganisms affect coral health and adaptation.

Coral Symbiont Transmission and Reef Interactions

- Some corals inherit symbionts from their parents (vertical transmission), while others acquire them from the environment (horizontal transmission).
- Fish like parrotfish play a role in dispersing coral symbionts by consuming coral tissue and releasing symbionts in their feces, aiding coral microbial diversity.

Ecological Complexity of Coral Reefs

- Coral reefs are rich ecosystems with numerous species interactions, including those involving fish, invertebrates, algae, and microorganisms.
- Studying these interactions helps understand reef ecology and the factors that sustain or threaten these environments.
