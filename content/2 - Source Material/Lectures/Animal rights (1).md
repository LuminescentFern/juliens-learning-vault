# Animal rights (1)

This lesson explores the historical and philosophical foundations of animal rights and welfare, focusing on how animals' self-awareness and capacity for emotions influence their moral consideration.

Historical and Religious Perspectives

- Ancient Hebrew tradition granted humans dominion over animals, often interpreted as a divine right to rule, though some argue it implies stewardship and responsibility.
- Aristotle and Thomas Aquinas viewed animals as inferior due to their lack of reason and souls, leading to indirect duties toward animals primarily to prevent cruelty toward humans.

Philosophical Developments

- René Descartes argued animals lack subjective awareness and cannot suffer, justifying practices like vivisection.
- Jeremy Bentham challenged the emphasis on reason, proposing that the capacity to suffer, not reason, should determine moral consideration, though he accepted animal use if suffering is minimized.

Animal Rights and Welfare Concepts

- Animals are recognized as self-aware beings with interests in avoiding pain and suffering, which grounds the argument for their rights.
- The animal rights view advocates protecting animals' interests through rights, while the animal welfare view focuses on minimizing suffering without necessarily granting rights.
