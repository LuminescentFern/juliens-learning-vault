# Geographic distribution modeling

This lecture explains the challenges and methods of documenting species' geographic distributions in ecology, focusing on niche-based geographic distribution modeling.

Documenting Species Distributions

- It is difficult for ecologists to observe species everywhere due to species' size, lifecycle, and limited observation points.
- Ecologists use known locations where species are found to extrapolate and predict other suitable habitats.

Niche-Based Geographic Distribution Modeling

- Starts with known species locations from field observations and records.
- Uses abiotic environmental factors like temperature and precipitation to identify similar conditions elsewhere.
- Produces maps predicting where species can potentially live, including past and future distributions.

Limitations and Biotic Interactions

- Models may fail when biotic interactions, such as predators, parasites, or competitors, influence species distribution.
- Example: Red imported fire ants in North America spread beyond predictions because they escaped natural enemies and have genetic mutations affecting behaviour.
- Parasitic phorid flies, which attack fire ants in their native range, are absent or introduced as control agents, showing how species interactions affect distribution beyond abiotic factors.
