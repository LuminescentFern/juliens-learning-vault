# Animal welfare

This lecture clarifies the distinctions between animal rights and animal welfare, exploring their ethical and legal implications.

Definitions and Perspectives

- Animal welfare focuses on the well-being of animals, advocating for humane treatment and accommodations without necessarily opposing human use of animals.
- Animal rights reject the use of animals for human purposes entirely, seeking to abolish practices like animal agriculture and pet ownership.

The Five Freedoms of Animal Welfare

- Developed in the 1970s for farm animals, these freedoms include freedom from hunger and thirst, discomfort, pain and disease, freedom to express normal behavior, and freedom from fear and distress.
- These principles have been adapted to various settings where animals depend on humans, such as zoos, shelters, and laboratories.

Legal and Social Considerations

- Animals are legally considered property in most countries, which has implications for ownership, care, and legal responsibility.
- Animal rights advocates challenge this property status, while animal welfare supporters see it as a framework for accountability.
- Research shows that support for animal rights often correlates with broader support for human rights and social justice issues.
