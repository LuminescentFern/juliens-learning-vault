# Animal rights (2)

This lecture introduces foundational philosophical perspectives on animal treatment and rights that have shaped the contemporary animal rights movement.

Peter Singer's Utilitarian Perspective

- Singer's 1975 book "Animal Liberation" argues for equal consideration of interests, emphasizing that animals' capacity to suffer morally obliges us to consider their suffering equally to humans'.
- He critiques speciesism, the bias favoring one's own species, and highlights suffering in factory farms and laboratories but allows for humane use of animals.

Tom Regan's Animal Rights Argument

- Regan argues that certain animals (normal mammals over one year old) are "subjects of a life" with inherent value, possessing emotions, desires, and identities.
- He asserts animals have rights to life, liberty, and bodily integrity, requiring abolition—not just reform—of animal exploitation.

Implications and Broader Context

- Singer focuses on reducing suffering, while Regan demands ending animal use entirely, including meat consumption and entertainment.
- The lecture notes complexities such as pet ownership and acknowledges other philosophical voices engaging with these foundational views.
