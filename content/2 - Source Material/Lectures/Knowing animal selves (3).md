# Knowing animal selves (3)

This lecture explores the concept of self-awareness in wild animals by examining evidence of the four components of the core self: agency, coherence, affectivity, and self-history.

Agency:

- Agency refers to an animal's sense of control over its actions and awareness of their consequences, demonstrated by animals making choices and engaging in social behaviors like reconciliation in spotted hyenas.
- Experiments with capuchin monkeys show reactions to unfair treatment, indicating awareness of self and others.

Coherence:

- Coherence is the ability to recognize oneself and others as distinct physical entities, as seen in elephants distinguishing between threatening and non-threatening human voices.
- This capacity grounds agency by locating actions within a physical body.

Affectivity:

- Affectivity links embodied actions with emotions, suggesting animals experience feelings associated with their actions.
- For example, a crow repeatedly sliding on a jar lid appears to enjoy the experience, indicating emotional self-awareness.

Self-History:

- Self-history involves memory that preserves the meaning of past events and interactions, evident in elephants' strong memory and birds' ability to recall food cache locations.
- These memories support planning for the future and a continuous sense of self.

The lecture concludes that while evidence of all four components in a single wild species is limited, the presence of these capacities across different species suggests animals possess forms of selfhood worthy of further research.
