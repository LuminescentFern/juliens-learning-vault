# Knowing animal selves (2)

This lecture explores the concept of selfhood in animals, particularly dogs and cats, by examining their behaviors and inner experiences beyond the use of spoken language.

Core Self Experiences in Animals

- Agency: Animals like dogs demonstrate agency by intentionally controlling their actions, such as figuring out how to retrieve a stick in water without swimming.
- Coherence: Animals recognize themselves as embodied beings, evident in behaviors like cats hiding to conceal themselves, showing awareness of their physical presence.

Emotional and Continuity Aspects of Self

- Affectivity: Animals experience emotions and vitality affects, which are emotional dispositions that characterize individual personalities, such as a dog being described as sweet or a cat as a character.
- Self History: Animals possess memory that supports a continuous sense of self, remembering places and experiences, which contributes to their ongoing self-experience.

Challenging Traditional Views on Animal Selfhood

- Contrary to George Herbert Mead's view that animals lack a sense of self due to absence of language, research on human infants shows core self experiences exist before language acquisition.
- Since mammals share similar brain and nervous system structures, animals likely have a core self, differing from humans in degree rather than kind.
