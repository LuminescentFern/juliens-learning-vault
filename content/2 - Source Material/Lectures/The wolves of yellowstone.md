# The wolves of yellowstone

This course content explains the concept of keystone species and their critical role in maintaining ecological balance, using the example of wolves in Yellowstone National Park.

Keystone Species Concept

- Keystone species are organisms that have a disproportionately large impact on their environment relative to their abundance.
- Predators like sea stars, sharks, jaguars, and wolves often serve as keystone species by controlling prey populations and maintaining community structure.

Wolves in Yellowstone National Park

- Yellowstone, established in 1872, lost its gray wolf population by the 1920s due to hunting.
- Wolves were reintroduced in 1995 as an endangered species, allowing ecologists to study ecosystem changes after their return.

Ecological Impact of Wolf Reintroduction

- Wolves reduced the elk population, which had grown excessively in their absence.
- The decline in elk allowed willow trees to recover along streams, which in turn supported an increase in beaver populations.
- Beavers created new habitats by building dams, benefiting many other species and demonstrating the cascading effects of a keystone predator.
