# Living with wildlife

This lecture explores the concept of living with wildlife in urban and suburban environments, focusing on how wild animals adapt to and coexist with human-built spaces.

Synanthropy and Synurbic Species

- Synanthropy refers to wild species that benefit from living close to humans, such as raccoons, pigeons, rats, and squirrels.
- Synurbic species are those that have higher population densities in urban areas than in rural ones, like pigeons and some insect pollinators.

Adaptations and Interactions

- Synanthropic animals adapt their diets, behaviors, and communication to urban conditions, including changes in flight patterns and courtship songs.
- These animals often live in close proximity to humans, utilizing resources like trash, gardens, and bird feeders.

Challenges and Human-Wildlife Coexistence

- Living near humans poses risks to wildlife, including collisions with windows, disorientation from artificial lights, vehicle strikes, and exposure to diseases.
- Urban planners and architects are developing solutions such as wildlife overpasses, bird-safe building designs, and strategies to manage animal presence safely.

The lecture emphasizes that the boundaries between wild and human spaces are increasingly blurred, highlighting the need to reconsider distinctions between nature and civilization as wildlife adapts to urban life.
