# The zoos connection with Houston toads

- **A Species in Need:** The Houston Toad was the first amphibian added to the Endangered Species Act in 1970. Due to rapid urbanization, drought, and wildfires destroying their specific pine-forest habitats, they are on the brink of extinction and haven't actually been seen in Houston since 1975.
    
- **Decades of Conservation:** The Houston Zoo has championed the toad's survival for over 40 years. In the 1980s, they started the very first Houston Toad breeding program to act as a "genetic ark" for the species.
    
- **Mass Egg Release:** While the Zoo used to release small "toadlets," they found a groundbreaking new benchmark for success: releasing mass quantities of eggs into wild ponds. Since 2013, the Zoo has released millions of eggs to boost the wild population.
    
- **Intensive Care:** The Zoo maintains an "assurance colony" of about 600 toads—estimated to be the bulk of the remaining species. Behind the scenes, specialized keepers and veterinarians carefully match breeding pairs, track genetics, and provide vitamins and hormones during an 11-week breeding season.
    
- **The Ultimate Goal:** The Houston Zoo's Toad Recovery Program has a clear, ultimate mission: to successfully recover the wild population to the point where the Zoo's program is no longer needed.
