# Ecosystem of wetlands

The _Sciencing_ article **"Ecosystem Of Wetlands"** details the unique environmental and biological dynamics of wetlands.Positioned as transitional ecotones—the meeting zones between purely terrestrial land and deep aquatic systems—wetlands feature saturated soils, distinct vegetation, and incredible biodiversity, while remaining highly sensitive to outside disturbances.

The article breaks down the ecosystem into four major components:

### 1. Classification and Types of Wetlands

Wetlands vary depending on their water source, geographic location, and dominant plant life:

- **Emergent Wetlands (Marshes and Fens):** Characterized by rooted, upright plants that extend above the shallow water's surface, such as cattails, rushes, and water lilies.
    
- **Swamps:** Forested wetlands dominated by tall trees and dense understory vegetation.
    
- **Bogs:** High-acidity, low-oxygen peatlands that accumulate moss. Due to their harsh chemistry, they generally do not support fish populations.
    
- **Temporary and Flowing Systems:** These include **vernal pools** (temporary pools filled by spring rains), **springs and seeps** (where the underground water table reaches the surface), and **riparian wetlands** (habitats located directly alongside flowing rivers and streams).
    
### 2. Crucial Abiotic Factors

The physical, non-living elements of a wetland dictate its entire structure, with **weather and climate** being the most prominent overarching forces. Key abiotic factors include:

- **Hydrology and Water Source:** Inland wetlands rely on groundwater and precipitation, while coastal wetlands are shaped by saltwater, ocean currents, and predictable tidal cycles.
    
- **Physiochemistry:** Water and soil chemistry—such as nutrient levels, physical temperature, alkalinity, and turbidity (water clarity)—play a critical role.
    
- **Substrate Composition:** The underlying bedrock directly impacts the chemical makeup of the water, and the wet, waterlogged soils create highly specialized, **anaerobic (low-oxygen) environments**.
    
- **Physical Dynamics:** Natural forces like wind-driven wave erosion, sedimentation, and ice scouring in colder regions constantly reshape the landscape.
    
### 3. Dominant Wildlife and Vegetation

Because of the abundance of water and nutrients, wetlands act as "biological supermarkets" supporting an extensive food chain:

- **Flora:** Plants must be specially adapted to grow in saturated, anaerobic soils.
    
- **Aquatic Life:** Wetlands are vital spawning grounds and protective nurseries for numerous species of fish, crustaceans, turtles, frogs, and snakes.
    

- **Mammals and Birds:** The habitat acts as a critical stopover for migrating waterfowl and provides foraging grounds for mammals ranging from minks and foxes to deer and bears.
    
    ### 4. Ecological and Economic Importance

The article emphasizes that wetlands provide unparalleled ecosystem services that benefit human infrastructure and the planet as a whole:

- **Natural Filters:** They serve as natural environmental cleansers, absorbing harmful chemicals, trapping sediment to improve water clarity, neutralizing bacteria, and filtering out pollutants.
    
- **Storm Barriers and Flood Control:** Acting like giant sponges, wetlands absorb heavy rainfall and excess runoff, providing essential flood control and serving as protective buffers against severe storm surges.
    
- **Economic Products:** They are highly productive areas that support global food resources, supplying essential commercial products like fish, rice, and cranberries.
    

The article notes that despite their immense value, wetlands remain highly vulnerable to **human intrusion**, frequently facing degradation from agriculture, shipping, and urban development.
