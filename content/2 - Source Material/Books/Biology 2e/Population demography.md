# Population demography

### 1. Baseline Metrics: Size vs. Density

Ecologists describe any population using two fundamental indicators:

- **Population Size (N):** The total number of individuals. Larger populations are generally more stable due to higher genetic variability.
    
- **Population Density:** The number of individuals within a specific area or volume. Low density can make finding mates difficult, while high density can spark heavy resource competition.
    

> 💡 **The Inverse Rule:** Generally, population density shares an inverse relationship with body mass. Smaller organisms (like rodents) are densely packed; larger mammals (like elephants) are highly spread out.

### 2. Field Methods (The Sampling Math)

Because counting every single organism in a massive ecosystem is rarely possible, ecologists use two primary statistical sampling methods:

#### A. Quadrats (For Immobile/Slow Organisms)

Marking off square areas (using wood, plastic, or metal frames) to count individuals, then averaging and multiplying those counts across the entire habitat.

#### B. Mark and Recapture (For Mobile Animals)

Capturing a sample of animals, tagging them, releasing them to mix back into the wild, and catching a second sample later to look at the ratio of marked to unmarked individuals.

$$N = \frac{\text{First Catch Marked} \times \text{Second Catch Total}}{\text{Second Catch Recaptured}}$$

### 3. Dispersion Patterns (Spatial Distribution)

How individuals space themselves out within their habitat reveals how they interact with each other and their environment:

- **Uniform:** Equally spaced apart. Often driven by territorial behavior (like penguins) or plants releasing chemicals that poison nearby competitors.
    
- **Random:** Scattered with no predictable pattern. Typical of plants with wind-dispersed seeds (like dandelions) that grow wherever they land.
    
- **Clumped:** Clustered in tight groups. Common in animals that live in social herds or schools (like elephants or fish) for protection and resource gathering.
    

### 4. Tracking Dynamics: Life Tables & Mortality Rates

Demography tracks how populations change over time by monitoring birth rates, death rates, and life expectancies. This data is structured using **Life Tables** to break the population down into specific age intervals.

To find the risk of death for any given age group, ecologists calculate the **Mortality Rate**:

$$\text{Mortality Rate} = \frac{\text{Individuals Dying During Interval}}{\text{Individuals Surviving at Start of Interval}} \times 1000$$

### 5. The Three Survivorship Curves

By plotting the data from life tables (the number of surviving individuals over time), ecologists categorize species into three evolutionary life-history curves:

- **Type I (High Late-Life Mortality):** High survival rates through early and middle life, with sharp declines in old age. Characteristic of humans and large mammals who produce **few offspring but invest heavy parental care**.
    
- **Type II (Constant Mortality):** An equal probability of dying at any age interval. Common in birds and some reptiles.
    
- **Type III (High Early-Life Mortality):** Very few survive their youngest years, but those that make it past the initial bottleneck live relatively long lives. Characteristic of trees and marine invertebrates who produce **massive numbers of offspring but offer zero parental care**.
