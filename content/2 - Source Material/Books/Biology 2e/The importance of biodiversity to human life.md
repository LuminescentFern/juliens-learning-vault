# The importance of biodiversity to human life

**1. Human Health and Medicine**

- **Pharmaceuticals:** For thousands of years, humans have relied on plants for medicine. Even today, many modern medications (like aspirin and certain cancer drugs) are derived from or inspired by natural plant compounds, animal venoms, and fungi.
    
- **Future Discoveries:** A significant portion of pharmaceutical research focuses on natural compounds. As species go extinct, we lose potential sources for new life-saving drugs.
    

**2. Agricultural Diversity and Food Security**

- **Crop Resilience:** Humans have bred thousands of crop varieties over millennia. High genetic diversity protects crops against diseases and pests. The tragic Irish potato famine is a prime example of the dangers of low crop diversity (relying on a single variety that fell vulnerable to a blight).
    
- **Wild Ancestors:** Maintaining the wild relatives of our modern crops is crucial. These wild species contain unique genes that scientists can use to breed new, hardier crop varieties that can withstand evolving pests and changing climates.
    

**3. Ecosystem Services**

- **Soil and Water:** A diverse array of microorganisms in the soil breaks down organic matter, maintains nutrient cycles, and preserves soil texture, which is essential for agriculture.
    
- **Pollination:** Biodiversity provides crucial natural services, such as plant pollination by bees, birds, and insects. Without these pollinators, growing many essential fruits, vegetables, and nuts would be nearly impossible.
    
- **Natural Pest Control:** Diverse ecosystems support natural predators and parasites that help control agricultural pests, reducing the need for costly and harmful chemical pesticides.
    

**4. Wild Food Sources**

- For about a billion people worldwide, aquatic resources (fish) are the primary source of animal protein. The loss of marine biodiversity due to overharvesting threatens global food supplies, drives up living costs, and permanently alters ecosystems.
    

**5. Psychological and Moral Value**

- There is growing evidence and a prominent theory (championed by entomologist E.O. Wilson) suggesting that human well-being is psychologically tied to natural, biodiverse environments. Furthermore, there is a moral argument that humans hold a responsibility to avoid inflicting unnecessary harm on other species.
