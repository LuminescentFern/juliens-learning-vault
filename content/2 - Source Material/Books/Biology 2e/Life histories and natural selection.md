# Life histories and natural selection


### 1. The Energy Budget & Allocation Trade-offs

Every organism operates under a strict thermodynamic constraint: **energy is finite.** Any energy spent on one function (like surviving a harsh winter) is automatically unavailable for another (like producing seeds or offspring).

Natural selection shapes a species' **life history**—the genetically determined schedule of growth, reproduction, and survival—to maximize evolutionary fitness within their specific environment.

Key evolutionary trade-offs include:

- **Fecundity (Offspring Count) vs. Parental Care:** Species that produce massive quantities of offspring invest minimal energy per individual, resulting in low initial survival rates. Conversely, species with low fecundity invest immense energy into protection and feeding, driving high initial survival.
    
- **Timing of Reproduction:**
    
    - _Early Reproduction:_ Maximizes the chance of passing on genes before dying, but often stunts the organism's physical growth and reduces long-term health.
        
    - _Late Reproduction:_ Allows the organism to grow larger and more competitive, but carries the risk that the individual might die before reproducing at all.
        

### 2. Reproductive Patterns: Semelparity vs. Iteroparity

Evolutionary selection produces two primary life-history reproductive schedules:

- **Semelparity ("Big Bang" Reproduction):** A species reproduces only once in its lifetime and then dies. They channel virtually all their stored energy resources into a single, massive reproductive event.
    
    - _Examples:_ Pacific salmon, bamboo, and certain annual plants.
        
    - _Environmental Driver:_ Highly unpredictable or unstable environments where long-term adult survival is unlikely.
        
- **Iteroparity (Repeated Reproduction):** A species reproduces multiple times throughout its life cycle. They budget their energy carefully to ensure they survive each reproductive event to spawn again.
    
    - _Examples:_ Humans, birds, trees, and most mammals.
        
    - _Environmental Driver:_ Stable environments where adults have a high probability of surviving from one season to the next.
        

### 3. r-Selection vs. K-Selection Theory

To organize these diverse strategies, ecologists categorize species along a continuum based on population stability and environmental pressures.

#### K-selected Species (Adapted for Stability)

These species are selected to compete successfully in crowded environments near their **carrying capacity (K)**.

- **Traits:** Large body size, long lifespans, late maturity, few offspring, and high parental investment.
    
- **Survivorship:** Closely mirrors a **Type I curve** (high survival through early/middle life).
    
- **Examples:** Elephants, oak trees, primates.
    

#### r-selected Species (Adapted for Instability)

These species are selected for rapid population growth rates **(r)** in unstable, unpredictable, or changing environments.

- **Traits:** Small body size, short lifespans, early maturity, massive quantities of offspring, and zero parental care.
    
- **Survivorship:** Closely mirrors a **Type III curve** (massive early mortality bottleneck).
    
- **Examples:** Marine invertebrates, dandelions, pest insects, rodents.
    

|Life-History Trait|K-selected Strategy|r-selected Strategy|
|---|---|---|
|**Maturity Age**|Late|Early|
|**Lifespan**|Long|Short|
|**Parental Care**|High|None / Minimal|
|**Offspring Size**|Large|Small|
|**Environment**|Stable, crowded|Unstable, uncrowded|
