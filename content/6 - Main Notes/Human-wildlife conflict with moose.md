# Human-wildlife conflict with moose

There are many good examples of human-wildlife conflict near me, but the most prevalent is human-wildlife conflict with moose. Moose are a leading cause of vehicle crashes in Newfoundland, with roughly 800 being hit every year. 

Since the Newfoundland Wolf has gone extinct, moose have had no real natural predators (Coyotes and black bears typically only hunt moose calves) so their population continues to grow. Moose will often wander into towns and yards, and they eat gardens and landscaping. Moose weigh up to 1300 pounds, and stand taller than cars. They feed on plants, shrubs, and aquatic vegetation (moose will swim under lakes in search of food, which is unknown and surprising for many). Moose are usually not aggressive, however they will attack in some situations. During rutting season the males will become aggressive and dangerous, and all moose will attack if they feel cornered or stressed. 

 Moose are difficult to see on the roads at night, and their long legs result in their body being what is hit by the car, usually sending it flying through the windshield. This leads to serious injuries and deaths in humans, along with wrecked cars and thousands in property damages. People will commonly hunt moose in Newfoundland, and the government has placed fences along parts of some highways to stop moose from crossing, along with moose warning signs. 
 
 The general public want safer roads, and are concerned about the danger of Moose. Rural residents and homeowners have to deal with moose on their property and want fewer encounters. Some strategies for resolution that have been suggested include increasing hunting (Government, hunters), building more fencing, (Government, safety advocates, general public), public education on the issue (Government), and Fertility control (Animal welfare groups). 
 
 Animal welfare groups don't like the idea of more hunting, But the Government supports it due to it being cheaper than many alternatives. Fencing is seen as effective for safety however it is also expensive and disrupts wildlife movement. Fertility control is humane however it is seen as unrealistic and much too costly. Education campaigns are widely supported however they have limited effectiveness alone. 
 
 There's no solution that's universally loved, and every fix trades one problem for another. In conclusion, moose collisions and other moose related issues are still a major problem, and their population remains high. The current approach to resolving the conflict is a mix of many things (more hunting, more road safety measures, more public awareness, etc.).
