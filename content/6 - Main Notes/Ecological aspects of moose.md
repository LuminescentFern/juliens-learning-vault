Category 1: Abiotic conditions that determine where the species can persist 

- Temperature/ Amount of light
    
- Amount of rainfall /Soil conditions
    
- Elevation
    
- Others
    

Temperature / light: Moose can survive in very cold environments because of their thick fur, large body size, and adaptations for the boreal forest. However, they are sensitive to extreme heat, so temperature affects where they can persist.

Rainfall / soil conditions: Rainfall and soil conditions affect the plants moose depend on for food. If an area is too dry or does not support enough trees, shrubs, and aquatic plants, moose will not have enough food.

Elevation: Moose can live at different elevations, but they usually prefer lower areas with forest, wetlands, water, and enough vegetation.

Other, water availability: Water is very important for moose. They drink large amounts of water, eat aquatic plants, and use water to cool down.

Category 2: Biotic interactions critical to the species survival

What does the species eat? Moose are herbivores. They eat leaves, twigs, shrubs, grasses, herbs, mosses, lichens, and aquatic plants.

What eats it? Moose calves, sick moose, old moose, or weak moose may be eaten by wolves, bears, cougars, and wolverines. In rare cases, orcas may hunt moose in coastal water.

What species does it compete with? Moose may overlap in food use with deer, elk, caribou, or beavers depending on the region, but competition depends on food and habitat availability.

What species does it form mutualisms with? N/A. Moose do not appear to rely on a major direct mutualism for survival.

Category 3: Population growth

- How does the species reproduce?
    
- How quickly does it reproduce?
    
- What limits its population growth?
    

How does the species reproduce? Moose reproduce sexually. During the rutting season, usually in late September to early October, bulls compete for cows through displays, sparring, and scent marking.

How quickly does it reproduce? Female moose usually reproduce once per year after a gestation period of about 7 to 8 months. A cow may have around 10 to 15 calves in her lifetime.

What limits population growth? Moose population growth is limited by predation, hunting, habitat loss, disease, parasites, vehicle collisions, climate change, environmental conditions, and their relatively slow reproduction rate.

Category 4: Ecological role

- What role does this species perform in its ecosystem?
    
- What are the consequences of its absence?
    

What role does this species perform? Moose shape the boreal forest by eating large amounts of vegetation. Their feces returns nutrients to the ecosystem and supports plants, fungi, and insects. Moose also create paths through vegetation and provide food for predators.

What are the consequences of its absence? Without moose, plant communities could change, biodiversity could be affected, predators such as wolves and bears would lose an important food source, and scavengers would lose access to moose carcasses.

Category 5: Impact of humans

- What activities performed by humans are affecting this species?
    
- Is the species threatened with extinction? What is its IUCN redlist classification?

What human activities affect this species? Human activities that affect moose include habitat loss from urbanization and deforestation, vehicle collisions, hunting, and climate change.

Is the species threatened with extinction? What is its IUCN Red List classification? Moose are listed as “Least Concern” on the IUCN Red List. They are not globally endangered, although some local populations may still be harmed by human activity, climate change, disease, parasites, and habitat change.

What actions can humans take? Humans can help moose by protecting habitat, reducing deforestation, managing hunting responsibly, reducing vehicle collisions, reducing climate change impacts, protecting wetlands, recycling paper, and raising public awareness.
