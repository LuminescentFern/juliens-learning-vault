# What ecologists are learning from indigenous people

The Yale Environment 360 article **"Native Knowledge: What Ecologists Are Learning from Indigenous People"** by Jim Robbins explores how mainstream Western science is increasingly turning to **Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK)**.

TEK is a deep, holistic understanding of local ecosystems developed and tested over thousands of years by Indigenous communities whose survival depended on its accuracy. The article highlights that while native populations make up only 4 to 5 percent of the global population, they manage lands that harbor 80 percent of the world's biodiversity.

The article breaks down how this collaboration is transforming ecological science across several key areas:

### 1. The Power of a Holistic View (The Arctic)

In rapidly warming regions like the Arctic, TEK helps scientists notice ecosystem connections they might otherwise miss.

- **Beavers and Whales:** In Alaska, Inuit hunters explained to whale researchers that an increase in inland beaver populations was choking off coastal salmon spawning streams, which in turn reduced the prey available for beluga whales.
    
- **Predicting Change:** In Finland, the Skolt Sami people are co-managing the Näätämö River with the government, tracking shifting insect populations and altering their own salmon fishing practices to help restore declining stocks.
    

### 2. Managing Ecosystems with Fire (Australia)

Western land management has long struggled with catastrophic wildfires. In Australia, scientists and land managers are studying Aboriginal "fire stick farming"—the ancient practice of using highly controlled, "cool" fires.

- **The Five Stages:** Aboriginal fire use focuses on controlling wildfire fuel, maintaining biodiversity, balancing species, ensuring food abundance, and predicting resources.
    
- **Raptor Behavior:** Indigenous oral histories led scientists to document that certain predatory birds (kites and falcons) will actually pick up burning branches to start new brushfires intentionally, flushing out prey into the open.
    

### 3. Sustainable Agriculture (Mesoamerica)

The article highlights the Maya people of Mesoamerica, who cultivate "forest gardens" around their villages. Researchers have found that these gardens are the most diverse domestic agricultural systems in the world, producing food while seamlessly integrating with and preserving the surrounding forest ecosystem rather than clearing it.

### 4. Shifting Scientific Mindsets

Beyond gathering raw data, the article suggests that ecologists are learning entirely new ways of perceiving nature. Indigenous frameworks generally view humans as an interconnected part of nature rather than separate from or superior to it. This worldview fosters an enhanced, highly trained mindfulness regarding environmental changes, as well as an enduring sense of optimism and hope despite the challenges of climate change.
