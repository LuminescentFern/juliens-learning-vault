# Applying island biogeography to all kinds of islands

The lecture explains the equilibrium theory of island biogeography and its broader applications beyond traditional islands.

Equilibrium Theory of Island Biogeography

- Developed by Macarthur and Wilson in the 1960s, it predicts the number of species on an island based on island size and distance from the mainland.
- The theory was tested with various organisms on islands worldwide and found to apply to any habitat "island" surrounded by unsuitable habitat.

Islands as Habitats for Different Species

- For land animals, islands are land surrounded by water; for aquatic species like fish, lakes act as islands surrounded by unsuitable terrestrial habitat.
- Example: Two nearby manmade lakes connected by a pipe allow fish movement, showing how isolation varies and affects species distribution.

Conservation Biology Implications

- Habitat fragments act like islands for species, with larger patches supporting more species due to more resources and higher colonization rates.
- Location matters: patches closer to other habitats support more species.
- Conservation strategies emphasize large preserves near each other and habitat corridors to facilitate movement and maintain biodiversity.
