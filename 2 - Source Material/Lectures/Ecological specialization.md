# Ecological specialization

This course content explains the concept of ecological niches, focusing on how species vary in their use of resources and the implications of these differences.

Niche Breadth and Species Types

- Niche breadth measures the range of resources a species uses; species with wide niche breadths are ecological generalists, while those with narrow niche breadths are ecological specialists.
- Example: Red foxes are generalists with a flexible diet and habitat range, whereas Texas horned lizards are specialists that primarily eat one type of ant.

Advantages and Disadvantages of Specialization

- Specialists depend heavily on specific resources, making them vulnerable if those resources decline, as seen with the horned lizard's decline due to the loss of harvester ants.
- Generalists face more competition but are less affected if one resource becomes scarce.

Niche Overlap and Ecosystem Implications

- High species diversity increases potential niche overlap and competition, leading species in diverse ecosystems like tropical rainforests and coral reefs to become more specialized.
- This specialization reduces competition but increases vulnerability to environmental changes, contributing to the threat status of these ecosystems.
