# Knowing animal selves (1)

This lecture explores the concept of animal selfhood and challenges traditional sociological views that self-awareness and inner lives are uniquely human due to language use.

Animal Selfhood and Tool Use

- The discovery that some animals, like chimpanzees, can make and use tools challenges the strict boundary between humans and animals.
- This raises the possibility that animals might also possess conceptual tools necessary for selfhood.

George Herbert Mead's Perspective on Self

- Mead argued that selfhood requires seeing oneself as an object and communicating this through language.
- Since animals do not use spoken language, Mead concluded they lack self-awareness, a view questioned by recent observations.

Evidence of Animal Self-Awareness

- The mirror self-recognition test shows that chimpanzees, dolphins, elephants, and others recognize themselves in mirrors, indicating self-awareness.
- Some animals, like Washoe the chimpanzee and Koko the gorilla, have learned sign language and can communicate complex ideas, suggesting language-like capacities beyond humans.

Communication Beyond Language

- Animals demonstrate forms of communication that convey inner states, such as gestures and expressions.
- Limiting selfhood to spoken language overlooks these subtle interactions that contribute to a sense of self in animals.
