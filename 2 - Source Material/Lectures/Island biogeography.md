# Island biogeography

This lecture explains the ecological principles of island biogeography, focusing on how species diversity on islands is influenced by island size and distance from the mainland.

Importance of Islands in Ecology

- Islands serve as natural laboratories for studying ecology and evolution due to their clear boundaries and simpler ecosystems compared to continents.
- Early naturalists like Darwin and Wallace used island observations to develop theories of evolution.

Species-Area Relationship

- Larger areas tend to support more species because they offer more resources and diverse habitats.
- This relationship is a fundamental ecological pattern observed by many biologists, including Edward O. Wilson.

Equilibrium Theory of Island Biogeography

- Wilson and MacArthur developed a mathematical model describing species immigration and extinction rates on islands.
- Immigration rates decrease as more species colonize the island because fewer new species remain to arrive.
- Extinction rates increase with more species due to competition and limited resources.
- The intersection of immigration and extinction rates defines an equilibrium number of species on an island.
- Island size affects extinction rates (larger islands have lower extinction rates), and distance from the mainland affects immigration rates (closer islands have higher immigration rates).
- Consequently, larger and nearer islands support more species at equilibrium than smaller, more isolated islands.
