# How and why we think with animals

This lecture explores how humans have historically and culturally "think with animals" by using them symbolically, metaphorically, and dramatically to convey meaning and express emotions.

Historical and Cultural Uses of Animals

- Early cave paintings in France and Spain depict animals, though their exact purpose remains uncertain—whether decorative, religious, or documentary.
- Animals appear in myths, legends, and religious stories, such as in Judeo-Christian traditions, highlighting their longstanding symbolic significance.

Animals in Modern Media and Culture

- Animals are frequently used in media, such as cartoons, memes, and magazine covers, to communicate humor and social commentary.
- Consumer culture employs animal names and logos (e.g., Dove, Jaguar, Twitter bird) to symbolize traits like gentleness or agility, while sports teams use animal names to evoke strength and ferocity.

Language and Anthropomorphism

- Animal references permeate language through idioms and expressions that often carry symbolic or violent meanings, though these are sometimes overlooked.
- Anthropomorphism, attributing human traits to animals, is a fundamental way humans interpret and relate to animals, enabling storytelling and moral lessons.

Characteristics Making Animals Effective for Thinking With

- Phylogeny: Animals closer to humans evolutionarily (e.g., mammals) are easier to relate to.
- Neoteny: Juvenile features in animals (cuteness) elicit positive emotional responses.
- Domestication: Familiarity with domesticated species (dogs, cattle) enhances their symbolic use.

Overall, thinking with animals helps societies share and understand important ideas and experiences, contributing to defining human identity.
