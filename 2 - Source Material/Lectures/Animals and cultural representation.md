# Animals and cultural representation

This lecture explores how animals carry symbolic meanings that vary across cultures and history, emphasizing that animals should be understood not just biologically but also through the traditions and stories humans have created around them.

Categories of Animal Traditions

- Animals can be seen as metamorphosed beings, capable of changing between human and animal forms, often appearing in myths as punishments or transformations.
- Divine animals represent gods or sacred symbols, such as Egyptian deities with animal forms or Christian symbols like the lamb and dove.

Symbolic Roles and Cultural Associations

- Demonic animals, like wolves, snakes, and black cats, have been associated with evil or witchcraft in various cultures, influencing human attitudes and treatment of these animals.
- Satirical animals appear in fables and stories to teach moral lessons or critique human behavior, exemplified by Aesop's fables.

Political and Broader Human-Animal Connections

- Political animals are used in literature and art to comment on social and political issues, such as Orwell’s "Animal Farm" and Spiegelman’s "Maus."
- Understanding animals as traditions connects us to a broader human heritage, allowing us to see animals as complex symbols that reflect human fears, values, and relationships.
