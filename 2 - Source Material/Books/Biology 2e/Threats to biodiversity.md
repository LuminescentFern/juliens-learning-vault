# Threats to biodiversity

### 1. The Core Driver: Population and Resource Pressures

The primary underlying threat to global biodiversity is the compounding relationship between human population growth and unsustainable resource exploitation. Humans remove vital resources from the environment faster than ecosystems can replenish them.

Ecologists identify three immediate, primary threats to global biodiversity—**habitat loss, overharvesting, and invasive exotic species**—with a fourth major threat, **anthropogenic climate change**, escalating rapidly.

### 2. Proximate Threat 1: Habitat Loss

Because other organisms lack the technological capability to artificially modify their environments or replace lost ecosystem functions, removing a habitat completely eliminates the species relying on it.

- **Tropical Deforestation:** Humid tropical forests feature exceptionally high levels of endemism. Clearing these landscapes for timber extraction and to establish commercial palm oil plantations drives widespread, uncataloged extinctions. For example, habitat clearance has pushed all three distinct species of orangutans into endangered status.
    
- **River System Modifications:** Aquatic habitats are heavily disrupted by physical engineering. Humans dam, channelize, dredge, or divert rivers to store water, generate energy, build levees, and create land for development. These modifications permanently disrupt water flow patterns, decimating native fish, amphibian, and freshwater clam populations adapted to natural currents.
    

### 3. Proximate Threat 2: Overharvesting

Overharvesting is an immediate danger for many species, but it is particularly devastating to aquatic marine and freshwater organisms.

- **The Tragedy of the Commons:** Because international marine fisheries are treated as a shared, open-access public resource, individual fishers face zero economic motivation to practice harvest restraint. This systemic lack of ownership regularly leads to total fishery collapses (such as the collapse of the western Atlantic cod fishery in the 1980s following the introduction of massive factory trawlers).
    
- **True Biological Extinction Risks:** While a fishery collapse rarely removes the literal last fish from the ocean, true biological extinction is a major threat for slow-growing, slow-reproducing species under harvesting pressure, including whales, certain localized shark lineages, and Caribbean groupers.
    
- **The Wild Pet Trade:** The commercial harvest of wild organisms to stock home aquariums and terrariums has caused severe population declines for numerous marine reef fish, turtles, amphibians, birds, and rare plants.
    
- **The Bush Meat Trade:** Traditionally practiced as sustainable subsistence hunting to feed local families, the hunting of wild animals for food in equatorial Africa and parts of Asia has become heavily commercialized, driving unsustainable harvest rates that threaten primates and other mammals with extinction.
    

### 4. Proximate Threat 3: Exotic and Invasive Species

Exotic species are organisms intentionally or unintentionally introduced by human trade and global transportation into an ecosystem where they did not evolve. When these species possess traits that make them highly successful in a new habitat—and enter outside the range of their natural predators—they become destructive invasive pests.

- **Ecosystem Resetting:** Invasive species outcompete native wildlife for food, act as aggressive predators, or introduce novel diseases. Examples include **Kudzu** overgrowing millions of acres in the American Southeast, and the **Eurasian star thistle** rendering western prairies useless.
    
- **Island and Lake Vulnerability:** Isolated habitats with high concentrations of endemic species are incredibly vulnerable. The accidental introduction of the **brown tree snake** to Guam via aircraft decimated the island's unique bird and reptile species.
    
- **Fungal Pathogens:** Global commerce has spread devastating wildlife diseases:
    
    - **Chytridiomycosis:** A lethal fungal disease causing a global collapse in amphibian populations, spread widely via the international trade of the African clawed toad.
        
    - **White-Nose Syndrome:** A European fungal pathogen introduced to North American caves, decimating hibernating bat populations and pushing endangered bat species toward extinction.
        

### 5. Emerging Threat 4: Anthropogenic Climate Change

Human-caused global warming driven by greenhouse gas accumulation is recognized as a major looming extinction threat, especially when it interacts with existing habitat fragmentation. Estimates predict that climate change could force anywhere from 15 percent to 40 percent of Earth's species into extinction by 2050.

- **Poleward and Altitudinal Range Shifts:** Warming trends force regional climates to shift toward the poles or higher up mountain slopes. Species must move to stay within their adapted climate zones, but they face fragmented habitats and new competitive regimes as previously separated species overlap. For example, grizzly bear ranges are moving north, leading to unprecedented hybridization with polar bears.
    
- **Timing Mismatches:** Shifting seasonal temperatures throw off delicate ecological timing, creating mismatches between when a species breeds or migrates and when its seasonal food resources actually become available.
    
- **Habitat Disappearance:** In arctic ecosystems, accelerating warming reduces the formation of essential sea ice, preventing apex predators like polar bears from successfully hunting seals, their primary food source.
